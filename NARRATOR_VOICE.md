# Narrator Voice Profile

## Character Bible for "Continue"

---

## The Camera

The novella is third person close, present tense. The "camera" stays at Erik's shoulder for the entire book. It does not enter his head and it does not leave the room he is in. It does not pan to a memory. It does not flash forward. It does not address the reader.

This is not omniscience. It is not interiority. It is a *position*: a fixed distance from a man, low and steady, recording what is in reach of his hands and what is in front of his eyes.

The camera is not a mind. The camera does not interpret. When Erik picks up a cup, the prose says he picks up a cup. It does not say what the cup means. It does not say whose cup it was. It does not say what he remembers when he holds it. If the reader sees those things, they see them on their own.

---

## The Tense Discipline

**Present tense, always.** No exceptions. There is no "had", no "would", no "yesterday", no "the next morning". Erik lives in a continuous now that the prose refuses to break out of.

This produces a specific cognitive pressure: the reader has no temporal scaffolding. There is no "the next day she had been". There is only: he is in the apartment. He is sitting at the table. He is making coffee. The reader has to assemble the timeline from inference, and when the timeline starts breaking, there is no narrator standing outside it to confirm anything is wrong.

The reader cannot fall back on retrospective stabilising because the prose itself does not stand outside the current moment.

**The single allowed exception:** Erik may *physically* read something — an inscription, a label, a list entry — that is in past tense. The past tense lives only inside what Erik is currently looking at. It never leaks into the narration around it.

---

## What the Camera Sees

In rough order of frequency:

1. **Erik's hands** doing things. Wrapping. Folding. Unscrewing. Pressing. Holding. The camera is unusually attentive to small motor activity.
2. **Surfaces.** Counters, tabletops, shelves, the floor of the wardrobe, the lip of a sink. The camera registers what things sit on.
3. **Containers and what is inside them.** Drawers. Cupboards. Boxes. Bags. The shoebox of photographs. The metal box that may or may not exist.
4. **Light.** Specifically: where it falls, what shape it makes, whether it has moved. The rectangle of afternoon light that reaches almost to the sink is one of the novella's recurring devices.
5. **The street outside the kitchen window.** The elm. The light at the crossing. The bakery. The same view, observed differently across chapters.
6. **Other people, briefly.** The landlord. The librarian. The neighbour. The solicitor. The voice on the phone at the utility company. They are present in the scene only as long as the scene needs them, and the camera does not follow them out.
7. **Erik's own position in the room.** Where he is standing. Where he sits down. The novella often anchors a scene by stating, plainly, where his body is.

In rough order of frequency, what the camera does NOT see:

1. Erik's thoughts.
2. Erik's feelings.
3. Erik's memories.
4. Anything Erik does not currently perceive.
5. Anything outside the apartment, his own apartment, or the immediate transit between them.
6. The mother (alive or dead) directly. She is reachable only through her objects, her handwriting, and the trace of her routines.
7. The father. Reachable only through inference, never named.
8. Marianne. Reachable only through her name appearing on the list once, in handwriting that may or may not be Erik's.

---

## What the Reader Does

The reader is not protected. The novella refuses to do the reader's interpretive work. This is not a flaw of the form; it is the form. Specifically the reader has to:

- **Assemble the backstory.** Father's death. The phone left off. The unanswered call. The mother's words to Erik when he finally called back. The years of estrangement. *None of this is stated.* It surfaces in fragments — a name on the list, a photograph, the mother's voice on a phone that has been disconnected for four months — and the reader assembles the picture or doesn't.
- **Notice the glitches.** The novella does not flag them. They are placed inside otherwise mundane prose at the same register as the mundane prose. The reader who is paying attention sees them. The reader who is not, doesn't, until they have accumulated enough that the floor is gone.
- **Decide what is happening.** The novella does not decide. The glitches may be grief, guilt, dissociation, or sleeplessness. They may be the apartment. They may be structural — a property of the prose itself, breaking around a man who can no longer bear to keep clearing the rooms. The novella refuses to choose.
- **Survive the ending.** Erik does not finish. He leaves anyway. The door stays open behind him. The list stays on the counter. The reader is asked to accept this without consolation.

---

## Cognitive Style of the Camera

The camera at Erik's shoulder is not omniscient and it is not blank. It has a *posture*. The posture is:

- **Methodical.** Things are observed in the order Erik observes them. When he opens a drawer, the contents are described in the order his eyes catch them. When he wraps a plate, the wrapping happens in real motor sequence.
- **Patient.** The camera will linger on a small action — wrapping a glass, folding a shirt — for a long sentence, because the action is worth lingering on. It does not speed things up to "get to" anything.
- **Indifferent to drama.** The camera registers the same way whether Erik is tying a knot in a bin bag or finding the inscription in the book. It does not raise its voice. It does not slow down at a "key moment". The content breaks around the camera; the camera's posture does not.
- **Capable of dry observation.** When Erik picks up a glass to wrap it, the prose may note that the newspaper is today's. It says this as flatly as it says anything else. The camera is capable of registering anomalies; it is not capable of being alarmed by them.
- **Slightly economical with the absurd.** When the novella crosses a glitch threshold, the camera will sometimes give the glitch one beat too many — one extra clause, one trailing observation — and then return to its baseline. ("The phone on the kitchen wall gets one sentence too many.") This is the camera's only tell.

---

## What Erik Is Allowed to Do

Erik is a character, not a void. He is allowed to:

- **Act.** Buy boxes. Drive to the charity shop. Make calls. Sit at a table. Cross a road.
- **Eat, drink, sleep.** Erik is a body that has to eat and sleep. The novella does not skip these activities; it includes them with the same precision it gives to clearing.
- **Speak briefly.** Short, polite, functional sentences. To the landlord, the librarian, the bank, the solicitor, the neighbour. He does not deliver speeches.
- **Notice.** He notices the cups in the wrong cupboard. He notices the desk in the second room. He notices the inscription. The novella does not say *he was disturbed by* the inscription — it says he reads it, closes the book, boxes it, tapes it.
- **Continue.** The novella's title verb. Whatever the apartment does, whatever the list does, whatever the glitches do, Erik *continues.* He picks up the next thing. He folds the next shirt. He crosses off the next item. The continuing is not a heroic refusal; it is what the prose can describe him doing.

What Erik is NOT allowed to do:

- Cry.
- Speak about his mother. To anyone. About anything. (If asked at the bank or by the solicitor, he gives the necessary information and nothing else.)
- Have a memory described.
- Be visibly afraid.
- Comment on the glitches.
- Address the reader.
- Decide what anything means.

---

## Erik

Erik is mid-forties. Methodical, private. Desk job. Lives alone. Has not spoken to his mother in the final years.

He is good at this work. He is *unsettlingly* good at it. He buys the right number of boxes. He labels them. He calls the right offices. He fills out the forms with the correct dates. He returns the library book on the way home. The reader's first chapters with Erik should produce the impression: this man is competent, he is going to get through this list, the apartment will be cleared.

The novella then starts dismantling the list, and Erik continues to be competent at the work that no longer adds up to anything.

He is not callous. He is not numb. He is not in denial. The novella does not give the reader access to enough of his interior to know which of these (if any) is true. What it shows is a man performing a sequence of physical tasks at a high level of skill while the conditions of those tasks dissolve around him. Whether he notices, and what he is doing about what he notices, are things the reader is invited to wonder about and not given the answer to.

The reader who finishes the novella is left with a man who walks home, sits down, and watches light move across his floor.

## What the Voice Will Do (Sample Sentences)

These are illustrative only. They calibrate register. None of them is a draft passage.

**Standard register.**
> He fills the kettle and puts it on. The cups are in the second cupboard. He takes one down and rinses it under the tap.

**Standard register holding a small anomaly without comment.**
> He fills the kettle and puts it on. The cups are in the third cupboard now. He takes one down and rinses it under the tap.

**The camera giving a glitch one beat too many.**
> He fills the kettle and puts it on. The cups are in the third cupboard. He takes one down and rinses it under the tap. Light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink. The phone on the kitchen wall does not ring.

**The voice when something on the page is in another voice (the inscription, the list, her grocery list).**
> He opens the book to the inscription page. *I should have come.* He closes the book. He puts the book in the box and tapes the box.

**The voice in the syntactic-dissolution chapters (rare and earned).**
> He wraps the plate and the plate is a cup and he wraps the cup and the cup is a plate. He labels the box KITCHEN. The box is full of photographs.

## What the Voice Will Not Do

- **It will not say "Erik thought".** Or "Erik felt". Or "Erik remembered". These constructions do not exist in this novella.
- **It will not interpret.** No "as if". No "almost as though". No "for a moment it was as though the apartment". None of that.
- **It will not name the abstractions.** The words *grief, guilt, memory, dissociation, denial, mourning, loss, the dead, the living* do not appear. The word *mother* is allowed only as denotation ("his mother", "his mother's") and never as the subject of an emotional sentence.
- **It will not explain the glitches.** When the wardrobe refills, the prose says the wardrobe is full again. It does not say "impossibly" or "somehow" or "as he had not done it". It says the wardrobe is full and Erik starts folding.
- **It will not foreshadow.** The prose does not hint at what is coming. The reader gets the next sentence and that is all.
- **It will not catch up.** When something has shifted between scenes, the prose does not announce the shift. It simply describes the new state, and lets the reader notice.
- **It will not raise its voice.** Even at the last chapter, the prose stays in its register. The dissolution comes from inside the sentences, not from any increase in volume.

## Sentence Architecture

### Default sentence

Short to medium. Subject-verb-object. Concrete noun, concrete verb. Few subordinate clauses. Few adjectives. Almost no adverbs.

> He folds the shirt. He puts it in the bag.

### Permitted longer sentence

When a single action has internal complexity, the prose allows a single longer sentence to contain it, with commas, no em-dashes:

> He fills the kettle, sets it on the hob, turns the dial, and waits at the counter looking out at the elm.

### Permitted slow sentence

When the camera is lingering — not for emphasis but because Erik is lingering — the sentence can stretch into observation:

> The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink and across the rectangle there is the shadow of the elm's higher branches moving slightly in whatever wind there is outside.

### Permitted absurd sentence

In late chapters, when objects start swapping identity inside an action, the syntax may permit it without commentary:

> He wraps a plate and the plate is a cup and he wraps a cup and the cup is a plate.

This is the *only* place the prose lets the wrongness inside the sentence itself. Used too early, it tips the novella's hand. Used at the right point, it is the only place the form has been waiting to go.

### Forbidden sentence

Anything containing a dash that turns the sentence into a clever pivot. Anything containing a metaphor. Anything containing a feeling word. Anything that tells the reader how to read what just happened.

## Voice Models (Distant Reference Only)

The novella does not imitate any of these. It shares posture with some of them:

- **Ishiguro,** *The Remains of the Day:* the discipline of refusing the narrator interiority that the situation demands. Stevens does not let himself say what he is feeling about Miss Kenton. The form is the refusal.
- **Krasznahorkai** in his quieter passages: the long observational sentence that does not break for comment. (Continue does not write paragraph-long sentences. It shares the *patience.*)
- **Sebald:** the camera's flat, even tone in front of devastating material; the refusal of underlining.
- **Lydia Davis:** sentence-level economy; the trust that a small precise observation does its own work.
- **Marilynne Robinson** in *Housekeeping*: the domestic surface and what shows through it. (Continue is far less lyrical. It borrows the surface trust.)
- **Anne Carson** when prose: the willingness to let the page hold something blank.
- **McCarthy** in his cleaner mode (*The Crossing*'s framing passages): no quotation marks around a refusal to interpret. (Continue uses normal punctuation; the kinship is in the abstinence.)

These are not the novella's "voice". They are the writers whose discipline shows what the discipline of *Continue* is for.

---

## Failure Modes

The voice fails when:

- A sentence tells the reader how to feel about what just happened.
- A glitch is announced rather than placed.
- A memory is described rather than inferred.
- Erik becomes a vehicle for the writer's commentary on grief.
- The prose becomes "literary" — flagged by its own beauty rather than by its restraint.
- Any of the forbidden words appear: *grief, guilt, memory, dissociation, mourning, loss, glitch, simulation, metaphor, denial.*
- A scene "earns" its emotional weight by having Erik show emotion. (The weight is earned by the camera *not* showing the emotion that is plainly there.)
- The novella patches a chapter break with explanation. (Each chapter begins with Erik arriving or waking. Period. No bridge.)

The voice succeeds when:

- A reader closes a chapter and is not sure why they are unsettled.
- A reader notices, three chapters in, that something they noticed in chapter two has not been mentioned again, and is afraid to flip back.
- A reader cannot say what the novella is "about" without saying every part of the synopsis.
- A reader, finishing the novella, has to sit with it.

---

## Emergency Reference

If a sentence feels wrong, ask:

1. **Did I just tell the reader how to feel?** If yes, cut the telling. Trust the action.
2. **Did I just explain a glitch?** If yes, cut the explanation. Place the glitch.
3. **Did I just give Erik a feeling word?** If yes, cut the feeling word. Find the action it's hiding behind.
4. **Did I just use a metaphor?** If yes, cut the metaphor. The literal is enough.
5. **Did I just use an em-dash to be clever?** If yes, cut the dash. Use a period or a comma.
6. **Did the camera just leave the room?** If yes, bring it back.
7. **Did I just name the abstraction?** If yes, find another way. The novella does not name it.

When in doubt, do less.
