# Example Draft

## Calibration Excerpts for "Continue"

These are short draft fragments used to test and calibrate the voice against the planning documents. They are not the novella. They are not even intended as preliminary drafts of the chapters they belong to — they are tests of whether the rules in NARRATOR_VOICE.md, STYLE_GUIDE.md, and THEME_DOSSIERS.md can hold across actual sentences before the full first draft begins.

Each excerpt names the chapter and the specific formal decision it is trying to demonstrate. Use them as calibration, not as canon. Anything here is revisable.

---

## 1. Chapter 1 — The Key (closing)

**Demonstrating:** Baseline register. The original 14-item list placed verbatim so that every later deviation is legible. The single "one sentence too many" about the wall phone that is Chapter 1's only tell.

---

He finishes the coffee at the counter. He rinses the cup and puts it back in the cupboard.

At the kitchen table he takes a piece of paper from the drawer and a pen from the jar on the counter. He sits down. He writes:

1. Kitchen — pack and clean
2. Cancel utilities
3. Bedroom — clothes to charity
4. Find the will
5. Living room — books
6. Sort photographs
7. Cancel phone line
8. Death certificate — copies
9. Bank — close account
10. Balcony — clear
11. Second room — clear
12. Return library book
13. Final clean
14. Return key

He looks at the list. He folds it once and then once again. He puts it in the inside pocket of his coat.

He takes his coat off the back of the chair and puts it on. He walks to the door. The phone on the kitchen wall is black, the kind with a cord, the cord coiled once around the hook below the handset, the handset on its cradle where his mother left it.

He opens the front door. He steps out. He locks the door behind him and puts the key in his pocket.

The stairs. The street.

## 2. Chapter 2 — The Boxes (the tape dispenser glitch)

**Demonstrating:** A gestural glitch sitting inside an otherwise normal wrapping sequence, at baseline register, uncommented. The next sentence continues without any pause.

---

The plates are in the cupboard above the coffee machine. He takes them down in stacks of four and sets them on the counter.

He tears a sheet of newspaper and lays it flat. He puts a plate in the middle of the sheet and brings the corners up and over. He presses the paper around the edge of the plate with the side of his thumb. He reaches for the tape on the counter and picks it up from the table. He pulls an inch of tape and cuts it with the dispenser's blade. He presses the tape across the seam.

He puts the wrapped plate in the box at his feet. He takes another sheet of newspaper.

The box fills. When there are twelve plates in the box he folds the flaps of the newspaper over the top layer, closes the box, runs a strip of tape along the seam, and writes KITCHEN on the side of the box with the marker from the counter.

---

## 3. Chapter 12 — The Inscription

**Demonstrating:** The one permitted break in the form. Past tense and Erik's voice are allowed inside the italicised passage, contained by italics. The prose around it stays in the present and does not react. Erik reads. Erik closes the book. Erik wraps it. The next four books.

---

The books are back on the shelves. He does not look at the titles. He takes them down four at a time and wraps them in newspaper.

On the third shelf his hand stops on a hardback. He takes it down. The cover is dark cloth, no dust jacket. He turns it to read the spine. He opens it at the front.

On the page before the title page is an inscription in his handwriting.

*I meant to come in the summer. I did not come. I know you waited. I don't know how to be in that room with you and I didn't know then either. I am writing this on the page because I cannot say it. I am sorry I could not say it. I should have come.*

He closes the book. He wraps it in a sheet of newspaper. He puts it in the box with the others. He runs a strip of tape along the seam.

He takes down the next four books.

---

## 4. Chapter 14 — The Rooms (the syntactic dissolution)

**Demonstrating:** The first permitted prose-level dissolution. Rooms that should not exist. The courtyard-that-isn't sentence placed flat. The permitted plate-cup-plate sentence using "and" chains rather than commas. A mislabelled box at the end.

---

He walks through the apartment. Kitchen, hallway, living room. Left into the bedroom. Right into a room with a piano.

The piano is an upright in dark wood, the lid closed, the stool tucked in. A music stand with no music on it. The room is small. The ceiling is high.

Through the piano room into a small study. A chair. A reading lamp on a side table. A cup of coffee on the side table, half-drunk. The window looks onto a courtyard. The apartment has no courtyard.

He puts his hand on the cup. The coffee is warm.

He walks back. Through the study. Through the piano room. Into the bedroom. Hallway. Living room. Kitchen. No study. No piano room. On the kitchen counter there is a cup of coffee. He puts his hand on it. The coffee is warm.

He goes back to the boxes and the newspaper.

He takes a plate from the cupboard and wraps it and the plate is a cup and he wraps the cup and the cup is a plate and the plate is in the box. He takes a cup from the cupboard. He wraps it. He puts it in the box. He labels the box with the marker. The word on the side of the box is WARDROBE.

He takes another plate from the cupboard.

---

## 5. Chapter 17 — Continue (closing)

**Demonstrating:** The first chapter's cadence restored. The three objects (paper, key, photograph) placed on the counter. The unlocked door. The bakery, the red light, the crossing. The final sentence about light moving across a floor.

---

He opens the front door with the key and goes in.

Kitchen. Hallway. Living room. Bedroom. Second room. Bathroom. Balcony. Her things are in their places. The bookshelves are full. The wardrobe is closed. The cups are in the cupboard above the coffee machine. The phone is on the wall.

He fills the kettle and puts it on. He takes a cup down from the cupboard. He waits for the kettle. He pours the water over the coffee and stirs it.

The elm is at the window. The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.

He drinks the coffee at the counter.

He puts the cup in the sink. He takes the folded paper from the inside pocket of his coat and puts it on the counter. He takes the key from his pocket and puts it beside the paper. He takes the photograph from the inside pocket of his coat and puts it beside the key.

He walks to the front door. He opens it. He does not look back. He does not lock it.

The stairs. The street. The bakery on the corner is open. At the crossing the light is red. He waits. The light changes. He crosses.

He walks the three blocks to his building and lets himself in. He climbs the stairs. He unlocks his door and closes it behind him. He hangs his coat on the hook in the hall.

He sits down in the chair by the window.

The light through his window moves across the floor.

---

## Notes on Decisions Made

These are the calibration choices in the excerpts above. They are revisable; if any of them is the wrong call, the correction ripples into THEME_DOSSIERS.md and possibly into STYLE_GUIDE.md.

### Chapter 1

- **The list is quoted in full.** The rest of the novella depends on the reader having seen the original 14 items so that every later deviation (crossed off without being crossed off, reordered, rewritten, replaced) is legible. An abbreviated list would weaken every subsequent list glitch.
- **The "one sentence too many" is the phone sentence** with the coiled cord and the final clause "where his mother left it". It is denotative rather than emotional — it states facts about the phone's physical configuration — but it is the only sentence in the chapter that lingers on a detail the chapter did not need to linger on. It is the tell.
- **The chapter ends on "The stairs. The street."** Two sentence fragments are permitted at a chapter boundary because Erik's body is doing actions in sequence. The fragmentation does not announce itself as significant; it reads as the prose going where Erik goes.

### Chapter 2

- **The glitch sentence is "He reaches for the tape on the counter and picks it up from the table."** The glitch is inside a clean coordinating conjunction — "and" — which is the least conspicuous place to put it. The next sentence ("He pulls an inch of tape") continues without a pause.
- **The chapter does not slow down.** The wrapping sequence continues for several sentences after the glitch, then summarises ("The box fills") and jumps forward.
- **KITCHEN is written in capitals.** Labelling in capitals is conventional packing practice and also sets up the later chapters' mislabelled boxes as typographically visible.

### Chapter 12

- **The inscription is six sentences.** Longer would tip into speech. Shorter would not hold the weight the form has been building. It had to include both a statement of what was not done ("I did not come") and a statement of the writing itself ("I am writing this on the page because I cannot say it") so that the reader understands the inscription is the displaced speech act it is.
- **"I cannot say it" is present tense; "I could not say it" is past.** This is the only place in the novella where the two tenses sit inside the same small passage. The pairing is deliberate: it is the form's single permitted admission that the unspoken thing is both then and now.
- **The prose does not react to the inscription.** After the italics close, Erik closes the book, wraps it, tapes it, and takes the next four books. The refusal of reaction is the entire point.

### Chapter 14

- **The plate-cup-plate sentence uses "and" chains rather than commas.** Commas would feel witty — a Lydia-Davis-like aphorism about identity. The "and...and...and" construction is what a man continuing his work would produce if the continuation itself were breaking. It is the sentence of someone not stopping.
- **"The apartment has no courtyard" is a single flat sentence after the description.** It is not commented on. The next sentence is the warm cup.
- **The warm coffee appears twice** — in the impossible study and then on the kitchen counter after Erik walks back and both rooms are gone. The doubling is the chapter's most compact temporal glitch and requires no gloss.
- **The mislabelled box says WARDROBE.** A box full of plates labelled with the name of a room of clothes. The label can change; what matters is that the word on the side of the box does not match what is inside.

### Chapter 17

- **The rectangle-of-light sentence is the one that must be word-for-word what Chapter 1 also says.** This is the entire purpose of the chapter's return to the first chapter's cadence. The sentence in the excerpt is: *The light from the window falls across the counter in a rectangle that reaches almost to the sink.* If Chapter 1 ends up using a slightly different version of this sentence, Chapter 17 must be adjusted to match — not the other way around. The reader's recognition of the sentence is the chapter's largest unspoken gesture.
- **The three objects are placed in a specific order:** paper, then key, then photograph. The order is the order of what Erik has been carrying in his coat pocket for the duration of the novella. The photograph is placed last because it is the most recent addition (Chapter 16) and the most weighted.
- **"He walks the three blocks to his building"** is an invented detail. The number of blocks can change. What matters is that the walk home is short enough to be a single paragraph and long enough that the reader registers the crossing.
- **The final sentence is "The light through his window moves across the floor."** It is a sentence about light moving across a floor, exactly as the synopsis specifies. It does not try to be a final sentence. It is the next sentence.
