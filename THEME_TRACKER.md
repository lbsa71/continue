This document tracks every motif, object, and structural device that recurs in the novella. For each item: where it first appears, how it recurs, what it does in the form, and what it must never be allowed to do.

## Detailed Motif Analyses

### The List

**First appearance:** Chapter 1 ("The Key"). Erik sits at the kitchen table after walking through the apartment and writes a numbered list of fourteen items on a piece of paper. He folds it. He puts it in his coat pocket.

**Physical description:** A piece of paper. Folded. Numbered handwriting. Initially fourteen items.

**The fourteen original items:**
1. Kitchen — pack and clean
2. Cancel utilities
3. Bedroom — clothes to charity
4. Find the will
5. Living room — books
6. Sort photographs
7. Cancel phone line
8. Death certificate — copies
9. Bank — close account
10. Balcony — clear
11. Second room — clear
12. Return library book
13. Final clean
14. Return key

**The recurrences and additions** (this is the spine; details belong in THEME_DOSSIERS):
- Ch. 2: Erik crosses off the first half of Item 1 and adds a sub-item.
- Ch. 3: Item 2 has a line through it that Erik did not draw.
- Ch. 4: The list is consulted normally.
- Ch. 5: A new Item 15 in his handwriting: *sort desk.*
- Ch. 6: Below Item 6, in handwriting that is his but looser: *call Marianne.*
- Ch. 7 (The Neighbour): The list is not consulted. The only chapter in which the list is left in his coat by the door.
- Ch. 8: Item 5 has a line through it that he didn't draw. He puts a question mark next to it. The handwriting of earlier items looks different from his current handwriting.
- Ch. 9 (The Bank): Sixteen items. He wrote fourteen, added one. Sixteen. Item 16 says: *the phone.*
- Ch. 10 (The Freezer): Item 7 has been rewritten. It says: *answer the phone.*
- Ch. 11 (The Solicitor): He doesn't look at it.
- Ch. 12 (The Inscription): Items reordered. Some he recognises, some he doesn't. One says: *the summer with the door.*
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): At his own table. Some items crossed off, some not, some he doesn't understand. Near the bottom: *forgive.* He cannot tell if it is his handwriting.
- Ch. 14 (The Rooms): A paragraph in his handwriting. Some words legible, some not.
- Ch. 15 (The Boxes, second time): He reaches into his pocket. Unfolds the paper. Her handwriting. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*
- Ch. 16 (The Photograph): He takes the paper out. Blank. Puts it on the table. Picks up a pen. Doesn't write.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): Placed on the counter beside the key and the photograph. Not consulted again. Left.

**What the list must never do:**
- Be remarked upon by the prose. The prose describes its current state. It does not say "the list was changing".
- Be lifted into the metaphorical. It is a piece of paper. Erik puts it in his coat pocket.
- Be summarised. The reader sees its degradation through individual instances, not through narration.

### The Wall Phone

**First appearance:** Chapter 1. The phone on the kitchen wall. The first chapter ends with one sentence too many about it. ("He locks the door. The phone on the kitchen wall." — or some equivalent inflection. The novella's only tell that the kitchen contains something that will recur.)

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 6 (The Photographs): He picks up the phone on the wall. Dial tone. Puts it back.
- Ch. 8 (The Death Certificate): The phone rings. He doesn't answer. It rings for a long time.
- Ch. 10 (The Freezer): The phone rings. He puts the glass down. Walks to the phone. Picks it up. Silence. Then a dial tone.
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): He calls the telephone company. The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago. He says it rang. He picked it up. The operator says the line was disconnected four months ago. He picks up the phone on the wall. Dead. Puts it back.

**What the wall phone is doing:** It is the unanswered call from the day of the father's death, transposed into the present and refusing to be either present or absent. The reader who has assembled the backstory recognises it. The reader who has not, feels it without recognising it.

**What the wall phone must never do:** Be named. Erik does not think about his father, does not think about the day of the call, does not think about his mother saying *why didn't you answer.* The phone is the phone. It rings or it doesn't. He picks it up or he doesn't.

### The Cups

**First appearance:** Chapter 1. Erik makes coffee with her machine and her cups. Drinks it at the counter looking at the street.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 6 (The Photographs): The cups are in a different cupboard. He opens the right one without hesitating.
- Ch. 9 (The Bank): One cup in the cupboard.
- Ch. 18 (Continue): The cups are back. He makes coffee. He drinks it at the counter.

**What the cups are doing:** A small, intimate, almost-trivial domestic object that the prose returns to in each glitch phase. The cups in the wrong cupboard is the gentlest spatial glitch. The single cup is the most desolate. The cups returning in the final chapter is the form's clearest signal that the apartment has either come back or that Erik has reached the apartment that was always there underneath.

**What the cups must never do:** Be commented on. Erik opens the cupboard and gets a cup. The prose does not say "as if he had always known". The reader sees the discrepancy across chapters and carries it.

### The Wardrobe

**First appearance:** Chapter 1, walked through. Chapter 4 ("The Clothes"), opened.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 4: He folds. He bags. He hangs things on a drying rack he finds on the balcony.
- Ch. 5 (The Will): Goes back to the wardrobe. It is full. The bags are gone. He starts folding.
- Ch. 9 (The Bank): The wardrobe is truly empty. Not cleared. Empty in the way of a wardrobe that was never full. The dry rack on the balcony.

**What the wardrobe is doing:** The wardrobe is the object that most clearly performs the central inversion of the novella: a thing Erik clears that returns full, then becomes truly empty in a different way. *Truly empty* is a worse condition than *full again*, and the novella places it later for that reason.

### The Inscription

**First appearance:** Chapter 8 ("The Death Certificate"). Erik thinks he has packed the books. The bookshelves are full. One is the book with the inscription. He opens it. The inscription page is blank.

**Recurrence:**
- Ch. 12 ("The Inscription"): The books are back. He packs without looking at titles. His hand stops on one. The inscription page is full. His handwriting. *I should have come. I know you waited. I don't know how to be in that room with you.* He reads it. Closes the book. Puts it in the box and tapes the box.

**What the inscription is doing:** The inscription is the closest the novella permits itself to direct emotional speech, and it is contained: it is *physically* on a page Erik is reading, it is in italics, it is in past tense, and it is *his own writing*. The membrane is everything. The novella will not break out of the camera-at-Erik's-shoulder posture into Erik's interior. But the inscription page lets Erik's interior leak onto a page that the camera can read along with him. This is the form's only permitted touch.

**What the inscription must never do:** Be analysed. Be repeated outside the italicised passage. Spread its tone into the prose around it.

---

### The Photograph

**First appearance:** Chapter 6 ("The Photographs"). A shoebox on the living room shelf. Erik goes through them. One photograph — him and his mother — is found in the bedside drawer and pocketed.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 6, end: The photograph is now in the shoebox, and the prose describes three people in it.
- Ch. 16 ("The Photograph"): He stays home. Takes the photograph out. Two people. Three people, a woman. Two again. Morning light, evening light. A garden, a kitchen, mountains. Holds it for a long time. Puts it on the table. From across the room it is one woman in a kitchen. Not smiling. Face open, patient. Looking at whoever holds the camera. Pocket.
- Ch. 17 (Continue): Placed on the counter beside the list and the key.

**What the photograph is doing:** The photograph is the relational glitch's most concentrated repository. Where the list shifts language and the apartment shifts space, the photograph shifts *who is in it*. Who the third person is, who the one person finally is, the novella does not say. The reader who has assembled the backstories has guesses. The reader who has not, sees a photograph that does not stay still.

**What the photograph must never do:** Resolve who the third person is. Resolve whose face the one woman has. The prose describes what is in the picture and does not name the people in the picture.

---

### The Grocery List

**First appearance:** Chapter 4 ("The Clothes"). Erik opens the drawer with the grocery list in her handwriting. Reads it. Puts it back.

**Recurrences:**
- Ch. 13 (The Phone Call): The phone bill is in the kitchen drawer where the grocery list was. The grocery list is gone.
- Ch. 15 (The Boxes, second time): He reaches into his coat pocket and unfolds the paper. Her handwriting. *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.*

**What the grocery list is doing:** It is the novella's most economical demonstration of the slope. The grocery list begins as a small object in a drawer that Erik briefly notices and puts back. It ends by replacing his list. The reader who notices the symmetry — that the grocery list disappeared from the drawer in Ch. 13 and reappeared in his coat pocket in Ch. 16 — feels the form closing.

**The four items:** *Milk, bread, fish, oranges.* These four words are the most domestic object in the novella. They will be in his coat pocket. They will not be commented on.

### The Crayon Drawings

**First appearance:** Chapter 15 ("The Boxes", second time). Back at the apartment after the charity shop. A room he hasn't cleared. A child's room: small bed, desk, crayon drawings on the wall. A house and a tree and a tall person and a small person.

**What the drawings are doing:** This is the novella's stable anchor in Phase 4. Everything else is dissolving. The drawings are exact, present, durable. He takes them down carefully, rolls them up, puts them in his coat pocket. The novella does not need to say what the drawings are. The reader knows.

**What the drawings must never do:** Be in his apartment in the next chapter. (They are not. They are in his coat pocket. They go with him.) Be remarked upon. The novella describes them, lets him roll them up, lets him put them in his pocket. It does not refer to them again.

### Marianne

**First appearance:** Chapter 6 ("The Photographs"). Below Item 6 on the list, in handwriting that is his but looser: *call Marianne.*

**Recurrences:** None. The name appears once. It is never spoken aloud. Erik never calls anyone of that name. The reader who has assembled the backstory recognises it. The reader who has not, sees a name on a list that does not match anything else and assumes it must connect to something they missed.

**What Marianne is doing:** She is the proper noun the novella allows itself for the deepest backstory. She is the friend the mother called when she could not reach Erik on the day of the father's death. The novella will not name this. The list does, and then never does again.

**What Marianne must never do:** Be explained. Be called. Be a character in the present action.

---

### The Bakery

**First appearance:** Chapter 17 ("Continue"). After Erik leaves the apartment. *Stairs. The bakery is open. The light is red. He waits. It changes. He crosses.*

**What the bakery is doing:** The bakery is the world. It has been open the whole time. The novella has not visited it because Erik has not visited it. In the final chapter, when Erik leaves the apartment for the last time, the prose lets the bakery exist for one sentence and then lets him cross the road. The bakery is the novella's final, unmarked gesture toward continuance: there is a baker, the bread is for sale, the light at the crossing is red and then green, life is happening.

**What the bakery must never do:** Be remarked upon. Be turned into a symbol. Have anybody in it.

---

### The Elm

**First appearance:** Chapter 1. The kitchen window faces the elm.

**Recurrences:** Ch. 6, Ch. 13, Ch. 17. Possibly elsewhere as the camera glances out the window.

**What the elm is doing:** The elm is a tree. The elm is, throughout the novella, a tree. It does not glitch. The light through its higher branches makes the rectangle of shadow on the kitchen counter that the prose returns to. The elm is in some sense the novella's calmest object: the thing the camera looks at when there is nothing else to look at and the room behind Erik is doing whatever it is doing.

**What the elm must never do:** Be the wrong tree. Have the wrong leaves. Be in the wrong place. The elm is permitted to be the same tree throughout. The novella's stability lives in it.

## Thematic Threads

### Thread 1 — Continuing as Substitute for Grieving
**Progression:** The list (functional) → The list (degrading) → The list (someone else's) → The list (left)
**Carried by:** Erik's competence at clearing, at calling, at returning library books, at filling out forms, at folding shirts; the prose's refusal to make him "stop and feel".

### Thread 2 — The Apartment as Accumulated Relation
**Progression:** Walked through (Ch. 1) → Worked on (Ch. 2-6) → Refusing to stay cleared (Ch. 4-9) → Containing rooms that should not exist (Ch. 12-15) → Returning to itself (Ch. 18)
**Carried by:** The wardrobe, the bookshelf, the second room, the inscription, the rooms.

### Thread 3 — The Failure of Documents to Confirm
**Progression:** The bills don't match (Ch. 3) → The will isn't where it should be (Ch. 5) → The inscription is blank (Ch. 8) → The middle name discrepancy (Ch. 9) → The line was disconnected four months ago (Ch. 13)
**Carried by:** The bills, the certificates, the will, the operator, the solicitor.

### Thread 4 — The Phone That Was Not Answered
**Progression:** One sentence too many (Ch. 1) → Picked up, dial tone (Ch. 6) → Rings, not answered (Ch. 8) → Rings, picked up, silence then dial tone (Ch. 10) → Dead, but rang (Ch. 13)
**Carried by:** The wall phone, the operator, Marianne (named once).

### Thread 5 — The Apology That Was Not Made
**Progression:** Blank inscription (Ch. 8) → Inscription full, his handwriting (Ch. 12) → *forgive* on the list, handwriting unclear (Ch. 13) → Blank list (Ch. 16) → Door left open (Ch. 17)
**Carried by:** The book, the list, the door.

### Thread 6 — Stable Things
**Progression:** The elm, the rectangle of light, the cadence of the first chapter — all return at the end. The crayon drawings — survive the dissolution and go in his pocket. The bakery — has been open the whole time.
**Carried by:** Anchors that the form needs the reader to be able to feel even when everything else is gone.

### Thread 7 — Acts of Self-Maintenance
**Progression:** Coffee at the counter (Ch. 1) → Breakfast at his own window (Ch. 7) → Bed (Ch. 7, 13, 17) → Coffee at the counter, again (Ch. 18) → Crossing the road at the right colour → Sitting down at home
**Carried by:** Erik's body. Erik's small repeated acts. The novella refuses to omit them and refuses to dramatise them.

## What Must Recur Where (Construction Checklist)

For each chapter, check that the required motifs are present at the right pressure:

- [ ] List state is correct for the chapter
- [ ] Wall phone status is correct (silent / picked up / ringing / dead)
- [ ] Cups state is correct (in cupboard / different cupboard / one cup / back)
- [ ] Wardrobe state is correct
- [ ] Bookshelf state is correct
- [ ] Photograph state is correct (when present)
- [ ] Elm visible (when in kitchen)
- [ ] Rectangle of light noted (when applicable)
- [ ] Inscription state correct (when book in hand)
- [ ] Grocery list location correct
- [ ] All glitches in the chapter are placed at baseline register
- [ ] No glitch is announced or commented on
- [ ] No anchor that should be stable has glitched
- [ ] Erik eats / drinks / sleeps if the chapter spans the moment
